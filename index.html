Abstract

Gaussian splatting reconstructions of buildings often contain significant background artifacts, floating geometry, and noisy Gaussian clusters that extend far beyond the structure of interest. While these artifacts may be acceptable when viewing a single scene in isolation, they become problematic when multiple reconstructed buildings are combined into a shared environment, where overlapping noise degrades visual quality and scene usability. Existing pruning approaches typically rely on external geometric priors such as LiDAR data or geometric consistency measures that require reliable scene geometry. In contrast, we introduce a lineage-aware pruning framework that leverages the Gaussian inheritance tree produced during reconstruction. By maintaining references to each Gaussian's ancestral lineage, including its originating MASt3R keypoint, our method enables novel analyses based on lineage depth, distance to parent nodes, and distance to the root ancestor. Using these lineage-derived signals, we perform hierarchical confidence propagation to estimate the likelihood that a Gaussian belongs to the target building or represents reconstruction noise. Experiments across multiple UCSB buildings and additional urban datasets demonstrate that our approach effectively isolates structures from noisy Gaussian splatting reconstructions, improving visual quality while preserving important architectural details.
